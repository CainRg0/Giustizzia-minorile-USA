The Juvenile Justice System in the United States

This website explains in a simple and visual way how juvenile justice works in the United States: when it began, which terms it uses, what rights minors have, and which measures are used instead of prison. All answers are written in English and supported by official sources.

Go to the answers See the sources

Origins

The American juvenile justice system is separate from the adult system and was created on the idea that young people need different responses, focused more on rehabilitation than on punishment alone.

1899

It begins in Chicago

The first court for young people was created in Chicago. It is considered the beginning of the modern juvenile justice system in the United States.

1967

More legal protections

The Supreme Court recognized that minors must also have important due process protections, such as knowing the charges against them and having a lawyer.

1974

Main federal law

The Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention Act came into force and is still the main federal point of reference today.

Different words and a different logic

The juvenile system does not always use the same terms as the adult criminal process because the basic idea is different: guiding the young person toward responsibility and change.

Juvenile system

  • Delinquency instead of crime in the adult sense
  • Adjudication instead of the classic trial with guilty or not guilty
  • Disposition instead of the traditional criminal sentence
  • Probation as the most common measure

Adult system

  • Crime / offense to indicate the offense
  • Trial as the ordinary trial
  • Sentencing for sentencing
  • Incarceration with a more punitive approach

Answers to the questions

The five research questions have been rewritten completely in English and each one has a clear, direct answer ready to present.

Question 1

What is the minimum age of criminal responsibility in this country?

In the United States there is no single minimum age valid for the whole country. The official OJJDP guide explains that in most states there is no minimum age to appear before juvenile court; in the states that do set one, the most common age is 10, although in some states it can be as low as 6 or 7.

Question 2

What is the name of the main law or organization that deals with young offenders?

The main federal law is the Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention Act of 1974. The most important government agency is the Office of Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention, also known as OJJDP.

Question 3

List three measures that are not prison.

Three non-custodial measures are: probation, diversion, and community-based services or programs, meaning educational and support interventions carried out in the community.

Question 4

What rights does a minor have when arrested in this country?

In the federal system, a minor must be informed of their constitutional rights and of the charges against them. Parents or a guardian must be notified, the young person must be brought quickly before a magistrate, and the process may include privacy protections such as closed hearings and protected records.

Question 5

Find one interesting fact or statistic about youth crime.

One very interesting fact is that only about 1% of young people are sent to adult court. This means that the large majority remain in the separate juvenile system, which is designed specifically for youth.
